# Research

## Working Papers

<div class="paper">
  <div class="paper-title">Relative Wage Misperceptions and Early Career Job Search</div>
  <div class="paper-authors"><strong>A. N. Christensen</strong>, N. A. Harmon, S. Settele and D. Skandalis (2026)</div>
  <div class="paper-venue">ROCKWOOL Fonden, Study Paper No. 279</div>
  <details>
    <summary>Abstract</summary>
    <p>Misperceptions about relative wages distort job search among early-career workers. Using a survey-RCT with 1,902 Danish job seekers linked to administrative data, we elicit beliefs about wages in three relevant jobs and compare them to actual administrative data. Eighty percent of perceived wage gaps differ from the truth by more than 50%, and two-thirds underestimate gaps, making lower-paying jobs appear too attractive. An information treatment causally shifts applications and actual wages. A discrete choice model implies that removing misperceptions would reallocate 9% of workers and increase wages by 1.2%.</p>
  </details>

<div class="paper">
  <div class="paper-title">Globalisation and the Gender Gap in Mental Well-Being</div>
  <div class="paper-authors">B. T. Arnarson, <strong>A. N. Christensen</strong>, B. S. Javorcik and J. R. Munch</div>
  <div class="paper-venue">Conditionally accepted, <em>Journal of International Economics</em></div>
  <details>
    <summary>Abstract</summary>
    <p>Working across time zones due to international trade increases the gender gap in mental well-being. Using Danish matched employer-employee data (2008–2017) linked to prescription drug records, we show that lower business hour overlap with trading partners leads to higher antidepressant use among women relative to men. The effect is concentrated among college-educated workers under 45 and is 3–4 times larger for single mothers. A five-hour decline in business hour overlap increases the gender gap in antidepressant use by 1.7 percentage points, representing a 57% increase in prevalence.</p>
  </details>

## Work in Progress

<div class="paper">
  <div class="paper-title">Family-Friendly Jobs and Family Outcomes: Evidence from Text Analysis of Danish Vacancy Data</div>
  <div class="paper-authors"><strong>A. N. Christensen</strong> and A. H. Jørgensen</div>
  <div class="paper-venue">Work in progress</div>
  <details>
    <summary>Abstract</summary>
    <p>How does job flexibility affect hours worked for workers and their partners, and what are the broader effects on family wellbeing and fertility? We apply BERTopic modeling to the full text of 2 million Danish job advertisements (2012–2024) from jobnet.dk, extracting approximately 300 job characteristics from 40 million sentences. Linking these text-based measures to administrative data on employment, wages, and family outcomes, we exploit within-firm variation driven by M&A events and CEO transitions to estimate the causal effects of family-friendly working conditions on labour supply, fertility, and job separations — including spillovers to partners.</p>
  </details>
</div>
